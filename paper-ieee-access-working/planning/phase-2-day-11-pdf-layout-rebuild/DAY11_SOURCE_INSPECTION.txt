  38: 
  39: The result supports two observations. First, the framework is able to convert raw pipeline symptoms into structured failure signals rather than treating all failures as generic execution errors. Second, the evaluation includes healthy and boundary-control executions, which helps test whether the detection logic avoids treating every unusual condition as a failure. This distinction matters in production settings because excessive false alarms can reduce operator trust and lead to alert fatigue.

  42: 
  43: Figure~\ref{fig:recovery-by-scenario} reports verified recovery outcomes across injected failure scenarios. Recovery is evaluated separately from detection because detecting a failure is not equivalent to safely remediating it.
  44: 
  45: \begin{figure}[t]
  46: \centering
  47: \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/figure_2_recovery_by_scenario.pdf}
  48: \caption{Verified recovery outcomes by injected failure scenario.}

  51: 
  52: The recovery results show how the framework behaves after a failure has been detected and classified. For recoverable scenarios, the framework attempts an automated response such as retry, fallback handling, quarantine, or controlled continuation. For scenarios where automatic remediation is unsafe or inappropriate, the expected behavior is containment or escalation rather than forced recovery.
  53: 
  54: This separation is important for evaluating self-healing systems. A framework that attempts to automatically repair every failure may create downstream data corruption or mask serious upstream defects. The recovery results therefore should be interpreted in terms of safe operational behavior, not simply maximum automation. A contained failure can be a correct outcome when automated recovery would be unsafe.
  55: 
  56: The recovery-by-scenario view also helps identify where the framework is strongest. Transient or structurally simple failures are typically more amenable to automated recovery, while schema and quality failures may require stricter containment depending on the severity of the violation. This supports a practical design principle: self-healing pipelines should combine automation with policy-aware safeguards.

  36: \begin{abstract}
  37: Reliable data pipelines support analytics, machine learning, compliance reporting, and operational decision-making, but failures such as schema drift, data-quality degradation, freshness violations, transformation defects, and corrupted outputs often require more than task-level retries or alerting. Existing systems provide workflow orchestration, data-quality validation, observability, incident response, and policy enforcement, but these capabilities are commonly evaluated as separate functions rather than as an end-to-end recovery lifecycle. This paper presents a reliability-aware self-healing control plane for data pipelines that separates failure detection, root-cause classification, policy-constrained remediation, execution, post-recovery validation, rollback or escalation, and incident evidence preservation. A reproducible synthetic experiment with 780 trials evaluates the framework across injected failures, healthy controls, and boundary controls. Under the current controlled experiment configuration, the framework achieved 100\% failure detection accuracy and 100\% root-cause classification accuracy, while verified recovery was achieved in 52.17\% of injected failure trials. The mean self-healing cycle runtime was 12.609 ms, with a median of 10.609 ms and p95 of 26.666 ms. The results show that self-healing data pipelines should be evaluated using separate metrics for detection, diagnosis, and verified recovery because accurate detection does not guarantee safe or validated remediation.
  38: \end{abstract}
